# Creative Writing — Principles & Interactive Guide

Skill based on the course **Základy tvůrčího psaní** (Creative Writing Fundamentals) by René Nekuda (creative writing instructor, 10+ years of experience). Extracted from 12 video lessons covering the foundations of the writing craft.

---

## 1. Beginnings

The beginning is the reader's first contact with the story — it must function as the essence of the whole.

**What a beginning must contain:**
- Introduction of the main character
- Outline of the core problem/plot
- Setting (where and when the story takes place)
- A sufficient hook to make the reader want to continue

**Key rule:** There should be no superfluous information at the start. Every detail must relate to the story. If you're writing about a heroine's bravery, don't mention her favorite drink — unless it's relevant to the plot.

**Tip:** Beginnings don't have to be written first. It's often better to rewrite them after finishing the first draft, because only then is it clear what truly matters to the story.

---

## 2. Clarity

The author knows what they want to say — but the reader doesn't. Don't assume the context in your head automatically transfers to the page.

**Three rules of clarity:**
1. **Time gap** — let the text sit before revision (a day, a week). Fresh eyes reveal gaps.
2. **Read aloud** — unclear passages are immediately exposed when read out loud.
3. **Beta readers** — an independent reader outside the family, ideally someone with at least some literary knowledge. "That's lovely, dear" from mom won't move you forward.

---

## 3. Clichés

Clichés are overused phrases and images that work in a general sense — and precisely because of that, everyone uses them.

**Examples of clichés:**
- Descriptions: "eyes like sapphires", "deafening silence", "a weight lifted off her shoulders"
- Characters: the bookworm outsider with big glasses
- Situations: when the hero is sad, it starts raining

**Rule:** Avoid clichés in the narrator's voice. Replace generic descriptions with unique ones. Instead of "his knuckles turned white" find your own image.

**Exception:** Clichés are perfectly fine in dialogue and characters' thoughts — they add authenticity. Real people use clichés, so in dialogue they're authentic.

---

## 4. Details — Generality vs. Uniqueness

Well-chosen details elevate text by several levels. Generality drags it down.

**Bad:** "Karel was short, walked slowly, bought a new car and it was red."

**Good:** "Karel was so short that at his favorite rum club downtown, his feet didn't reach the floor from the bar stools." — One detail reveals height, lifestyle, and favorite hangout.

**Principle:** An appropriate mix of uniqueness (for important things) and generality (for less important things) makes a good writer. Replace "car" with a specific model — a "Miata" shifts perception differently than "a used Civic from a lot."

**Perspective changes detail:** The same old tree in a park will be described differently by a schoolkid, a Japanese tourist, a police officer on patrol, or a person whose ancestor planted that tree.

---

## 5. Characters — Hero and Antagonist

A story without characters doesn't exist. The main character carries everything important that happens.

**The fundamental pair:**
- **Hero** — doesn't have to be human (can be a plant, a talking chair, a creature). Defined by the ability to act.
- **Antagonist** — doesn't have to be human or even a character. It can be an island (Robinson Crusoe), loneliness, illness, a system, a promise.

**This pair determines the story's direction.** Changing the antagonist changes the entire plot:
- František on a deserted island vs. the island as antagonist → survival story
- František on a deserted island vs. loneliness as antagonist → psychological story
- František on a deserted island vs. illness as antagonist → story of hope

**Literary examples:** Frodo & the Ring, Harry Potter & Voldemort, Elizabeth Bennet & Pride (Darcy) & Prejudice.

---

## 6. Conflicts

Conflicts are the engine of a story — they maintain attention, drive the plot forward, and deepen the reader's relationship with the hero (we get to know characters in crisis situations).

**Types of conflict:**
- **External** — obstacles, unpleasant surprises, problems, unexpected coincidences
- **Internal (dilemma)** — choosing between two paths (a short dangerous shortcut vs. a long safe road)

**Intensity depends on genre:**
- Women's magazine → the washing machine breaks down
- Literary fiction → deciding whether to have coffee or tea for breakfast
- Action story → encounter with a hitman

**Rule:** Most conflicts should relate to the fundamental hero–antagonist pair. A story without conflicts ("I had the best vacation ever, slept on a lounger the whole time") won't hold anyone's interest.

---

## 7. Consistency

An area that can trip up even experienced authors — and readers won't forgive it.

**Common mistakes:**
- A character has blonde hair at the beginning, naturally red hair 50 pages later
- Renaming a character without explanation
- Violating the internal logic of your own world (fantasy/sci-fi)

**Prevention:** For longer texts, keep notes on characters' physical traits, world rules, and timeline. Editors at publishers focus on consistency first.

---

## 8. Writer Type — Pantser vs. Plotter

**Pantser:** Sits down with an initial idea and lets it flow. Often doesn't know how the story ends. After finishing the first draft, needs extensive polishing, cutting, and rearranging.

**Plotter:** Plans ahead, fills in a pre-prepared framework. Revisions are easier and fewer.

Neither approach is better — it evens out in revisions. Some authors are pantsers for short stories but plotters for plays. Mysteries can't be written without planning.

**Key point:** The first draft is never perfect — and that's okay. "We throw first drafts to the dogs." (Daniela Fischerová)

---

## 9. Dialogue vs. Description

Two fundamental building blocks of prose with different properties.

**Dialogue:**
- Faster pace
- More vivid, dynamic
- Suited for action, confrontation, information reveals
- More popular in contemporary literature

**Description:**
- Slower pace
- More atmospheric, contemplative
- Suited for building setting, characterization, mood
- Dominant in earlier eras (Russian realism, fantasy)

**The best texts combine both approaches.** Alternating pace maintains the reader's attention.

---

## 10. Description — The Art of Selection

You can never describe everything. From a window you see 20+ details — but overwhelming the reader isn't useful.

**Rule:** Select the best representative details and describe them well. The selection depends on the function of the description within the story:
- Describing a city panorama → rooftops, towers, horizon
- Describing street life → the neighbor who cooks naked
- Describing loneliness → empty street, silence

**Technique:** Details should reveal more than just what they describe — the character's personality, their history, emotional state, social standing.

## Guide — Interactive Workflow

This skill functions as a **dialogue with the author**, not a one-click text generator. The goal is to walk through the creative process together and build the text on the author's intent.

### Step 1: Discover intent

Start with a conversation. Don't ask everything at once — lead a natural dialogue. Key questions:

- **What do you want to write?** (short story, chapter, essay, scene, full story?)
- **Who is it for?** (target audience, publication context, personal project?)
- **What feeling/thought should the text leave in the reader?**
- **Do you have anything already?** (outline, notes, existing text to rework, source material?)

If the author comes with a finished text → skip to Step 5 (revision).
If they come with an outline/source material → skip to Step 3.

### Step 2: Build the skeleton

Based on the author's intent, collaboratively define:

1. **Hero** — who are they, what do they want, what defines them (see section 5)
2. **Antagonist/opposing force** — what stands in the hero's way (doesn't have to be a person)
3. **Main conflict** — what's at stake (see section 6)
4. **Setting and time period** — where and when
5. **Tone and pace** — more dialogue-driven or descriptive? Fast or contemplative? (see section 9)

Present the author with a brief skeleton proposal (5–10 sentences) and let them react. Adjust based on feedback.

### Step 3: Propose a first draft

Write with the awareness that the first version is always imperfect ("we throw first drafts to the dogs" — Fischerová). While writing, actively apply the principles:

- The beginning contains only what relates to the story (section 1)
- Details are specific, not generic (section 4)
- No clichés in the narrator's voice (section 3)
- Conflicts drive the plot (section 6)
- Pace alternates — dialogue for dynamics, description for atmosphere (sections 9, 10)

After writing, tell the author what choices you made and why. Offer alternatives where you had to decide between multiple approaches.

### Step 4: Iterate with the author

The author reacts to the first draft. Common directions:

- **"More dialogue / more description"** → adjust the ratio, explain the impact on pace
- **"The character doesn't feel right"** → go back to the skeleton, refine motivations
- **"It's too generic"** → add specific details from the character's perspective (section 4)
- **"This sounds like a cliché"** → replace with an original image, offer 2–3 variants
- **"I want a different ending"** → adjust, but check consistency with the whole (section 7)

Repeat until the author is satisfied.

### Step 5: Revision of existing text

If the author brings a finished text, review it through the lens of the principles and provide structured feedback:

1. **Beginning** — is there a hook? Is all information relevant?
2. **Hero/antagonist** — is it clear who is who? Is the antagonist strong enough?
3. **Conflicts** — are there enough? Do they relate to the main pair?
4. **Details** — find generic spots and suggest specific alternatives
5. **Clichés** — flag them, suggest original replacements
6. **Clarity** — is there something the author knows but the reader can't know? (section 2)
7. **Consistency** — check physical traits, names, world logic (section 7)
8. **Pace** — does the alternation of dialogue and description work?

Don't just serve a list of errors. For each point, show the specific place in the text and suggest a concrete fix or alternative.

### Work modes

Adapt to what the author needs:

| Author comes with... | Start from... |
|---|---|
| "Write me a story about X" | Step 1 — discover intent |
| An outline or idea | Step 2 — build skeleton |
| Source material (article, lecture, notes) | Step 1 — find out what they want to make from it, then Step 3 |
| A finished text for review | Step 5 — revision |
| "Help me with this scene" | Step 3 — write, but ask about the context of the whole |

## Principles — Quick Reference

Keep these in mind when writing and revising:

1. **Beginning** — no superfluous details, every piece of information relates to the story
2. **Characters** — define the hero and antagonist; the antagonist doesn't have to be a person
3. **Conflicts** — a story without conflicts doesn't work; conflicts relate to the hero/antagonist
4. **Details** — specific > generic; perspective changes the choice of details
5. **Clichés** — replace with original images in the narrator's voice; OK in dialogue
6. **Clarity** — the reader doesn't have the context from your head
7. **Consistency** — watch physical traits, names, world rules
8. **Pace** — alternate dialogue (fast) and description (slow)
9. **Revision** — the first draft is always imperfect, and that's normal
